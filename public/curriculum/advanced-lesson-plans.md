### Lesson 9.1: Argument Structure & Logic

**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Level:** B1-B2

#### Warmup (5 min)
- Review: Intermediate opinion expression
- "What's your position on [current event]?"

#### Core Content (35 min)

**The Complete Argument Structure:**

**1. CLAIM (Your position)**
```
"I believe that climate change is the biggest threat to our generation."
"The evidence suggests that remote work is more productive."
```

**2. REASONING (Why you believe it)**
```
"This is because multiple studies show..."
"The reason I think this is..."
"Evidence indicates..."
```

**3. EVIDENCE (Facts/examples supporting it)**
```
"For example, recent data from Stanford shows..."
"Consider: last year's reports demonstrated..."
"Statistical evidence points to..."
```

**4. COUNTERARGUMENT (Acknowledge the other side)**
```
"Admittedly, some argue that..."
"It's fair to point out that..."
"One might contend that..."
```

**5. CONCESSION & REBUTTAL (Agree where reasonable, then refute)**
```
"While that's a valid point, the data still shows..."
"I acknowledge that perspective, however, the evidence is overwhelming."
"That may be true in certain contexts, but overall..."
```

**6. CONCLUSION (Reinforce your position)**
```
"Given all this evidence, it's clear that..."
"Taking all factors into account, my position is..."
"In light of this analysis, the conclusion seems inescapable."
```

**Example: Complete Argument**

```
CLAIM: "Remote work should be the default option for most jobs."

REASONING: "This is because it offers several advantages that traditional offices don't."

EVIDENCE: 
- "Studies from Gartner (2023) show 78% of remote workers reported higher productivity"
- "Companies like GitLab save $50M annually on real estate"
- "Employees report better work-life balance"

COUNTERARGUMENT: 
"Critics argue that remote work damages company culture and collaboration."

CONCESSION & REBUTTAL:
"While those are legitimate concerns, evidence suggests otherwise. Companies with strong remote cultures, like Buffer, actually report higher employee retention and satisfaction. The key is intentional communication design."

CONCLUSION:
"The benefits substantially outweigh the risks. Evidence strongly supports making remote work the standard."
```

#### Speaking Practice (20 min)

**Exercise 1: Argument Deconstruction**
- Teacher provides argument
- Student identifies: claim, evidence, counterargument, etc.
- Discussion: Is it persuasive? Why?

**Exercise 2: Build Argument Step-by-Step**
- Topic: "Should AI be regulated by governments?"
- Student provides: claim, reasoning, evidence, counterargument, rebuttal
- Teacher coaches each component

**Exercise 3: 5-Minute Persuasive Speech**
- Topic of student's choice
- Must include all 6 components
- Delivered conversationally (not written-like)
- Teacher gives feedback on logic and persuasiveness

**Exercise 4: Live Debate (Simple)**
- Topic decided in advance
- Student argues one position
- Teacher argues opposite position
- Student must defend + rebut with evidence

#### Homework (5 min)
- Write outline of 3-minute argument on topic of interest
- Include evidence sources
- Practice delivery with natural pacing
- Prepare for next lesson debate

### Lesson 9.2: Hedging Language & Diplomatic Expression

**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Level:** B1-B2

#### Warmup (5 min)
- Argument structure review from 9.1

#### Core Content (30 min)

**What is Hedging?**
Making claims sound less absolute when appropriate. Used in academic, professional, and diplomatic contexts.

**STRONG CLAIMS vs. HEDGED CLAIMS:**

**STRONG (not always appropriate):**
- "Remote work is better than office work."
- "This policy will fail."
- "Everyone should exercise daily."

**HEDGED (more nuanced):**
- "Remote work appears to work better for many professionals."
- "This policy may encounter significant challenges."
- "Regular physical activity would benefit most people."

**Hedging Devices:**

**Modal Verbs (softening certainty):**
```
I know this is true           → It may/might/could be true
I think this is definitely... → This tends to be...
This will happen             → This might happen
```

**Adverbs of Degree:**
```
Entirely → Arguably
Completely → Largely
Always → Often, typically, frequently
```

**Qualifiers:**
```
"In most cases..."
"Generally speaking..."
"It could be argued that..."
"There's a reasonable case for..."
"To some extent..."
"In certain contexts..."
```

**Example of Hedging - Same Content, Different Tone:**

**AGGRESSIVE (Inappropriate for professional setting):**
```
"Your proposal is completely wrong. It will never work. 
Everyone can see the flaws."
```

**PROFESSIONAL (Hedged appropriately):**
```
"Your proposal has some merit, though there are some potential challenges 
I'd like to discuss. Some stakeholders may have concerns about implementation. 
Have you considered...?"
```

**Cultural Contexts:**
- Asian cultures: More hedging generally
- American culture: Less hedging, more direct
- British culture: Heavy hedging, understatement
- Latin cultures: More expressive, less hedging

**When NOT to Hedge (Be Direct):**
- Safety: "This equipment is dangerous."
- Legal/Professional: "This contract is binding."
- Urgent matters: "We need to act now."

#### Speaking Practice (20 min)

**Exercise 1: Aggressive → Hedged**
- Teacher gives aggressive statement
- Student converts to professional/hedged version
- Goal: Same content, softer tone

Examples:
- "That idea is stupid." → "That approach might present some challenges because..."
- "You're wrong." → "I see your perspective. However, I might suggest..."
- "This will definitely fail." → "There may be some risks worth considering..."

**Exercise 2: Context-Appropriate Hedging**
- Different scenarios (professional meeting, friendship, emergency) 
- Student adjusts hedging level appropriately
- "We should probably..." (friend) vs. "We must..." (safety)

**Exercise 3: Academic/Professional Presentation with Hedging**
- Topic: Research finding or professional update
- Student presents using:
  - Appropriate hedging for claims
  - Modals (may, might, could)
  - Qualifiers (largely, arguably, tends to)
  - Shows nuance and careful analysis

**Exercise 4: Diplomatic Disagreement**
- Student disagrees with teacher's position
- Uses hedging language throughout
- Maintains respect + openness
- "While I appreciate that perspective, I'd offer that..."

#### Homework (5 min)
- Record 2-3 minute professional response
- Include hedged claims
- Use modal verbs, qualifiers, appropriate to context
- Practice diplomatic tone

### Lesson 9.3: Persuasion Techniques & Fallacy Recognition

**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Level:** B1-B2

#### Warmup (5 min)
- Hedging practice from 9.2

#### Core Content (30 min)

**Persuasion Techniques (Ethical):**

**1. APPEAL TO LOGIC (Logos)**
- Factual evidence, statistical data, research
- "A 2023 Stanford survey of 10,000 workers shows..."
- Most persuasive in academic/professional contexts

**2. APPEAL TO EMOTION (Pathos)**
- Stories, relatable examples, values
- "Imagine being unable to afford healthcare for your child..."
- Powerful but must be authentic

**3. APPEAL TO CREDIBILITY (Ethos)**
- Expert opinion, personal experience, authority
- "Dr. Smith, who has 30 years of research..."
- "As someone who lived through..."

**4. REPETITION & EMPHASIS**
- Hammering home key points
- "This is crucial. Let me say it again..."
- Works for memorable messages

**Common Persuasion Fallacies (To Avoid or Recognize):**

**AD HOMINEM:**
```
FALLACY: "You're wrong because you're stupid."
CORRECT: Address the argument, not the person
```

**STRAWMAN:**
```
FALLACY: 
Person A: "We should invest in public transportation"
Person B: "You want to spend all our money on buses!"
CORRECT: Engage with actual argument
```

**FALSE DICHOTOMY:**
```
FALLACY: "You're either with us or against us."
CORRECT: "There are multiple valid positions here..."
```

**APPEAL TO AUTHORITY (Misused):**
```
FALLACY: "A celebrity said so, therefore it's true."
CORRECT: "Peer-reviewed research shows..."
```

**BANDWAGON:**
```
FALLACY: "Everyone believes this, so it must be true."
CORRECT: "Evidence shows X, and here's why that matters..."
```

#### Speaking Practice (20 min)

**Exercise 1: Identify Persuasion Techniques**
- Teacher makes argument using technique
- Student identifies: Logic? Emotion? Credibility?
- Discussion: Is it effective? Why?

**Exercise 2: Persuasive Speech with Multiple Techniques**
- Topic: Convince someone to adopt a behavior (exercise, reading, language learning)
- Must include: logic, emotion, credibility
- 3-4 minutes
- Teacher assesses balance of techniques

**Exercise 3: Recognize & Counter Fallacies**
- Teacher makes argument containing fallacy
- Student identifies fallacy
- Student provides correct counter-argument
- Examples: strawman, false dichotomy, bandwagon

**Exercise 4: Persuade on Neutral Topic**
- Topic: New policy at work, website design, curriculum change
- Student must persuade skeptical listener
- Uses ethics, logic, emotion appropriately
- Avoids fallacies

#### Homework (5 min)
- Record persuasive 2-minute argument
- Include at least 2 persuasion techniques
- Demonstrate no logical fallacies
- Goal: Ethical, effective persuasion

### Lesson 10.1: Phrasal Verbs & Idiomatic Expressions

**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Level:** B1-B2

#### Warmup (5 min)
- Quick debate follow-up from 9.4

#### Core Content (30 min)

**What's a Phrasal Verb?**
Verb + preposition/adverb = different meaning

**EXAMPLES:**
```
Look (see something)
vs.
Look up (research, improve)
vs.
Look down on (disrespect)
```

**Common Phrasal Verbs (Advanced):**
```
FINISH/COMPLETE:
- Wrap up
- Round out
- Tie up loose ends

FAIL/STOP:
- Fall through
- Back out
- Give up on
- Walk away from

CONTINUE/PERSIST:
- Keep at it
- Stick with
- Persevere in
- Hang in there

IMPROVE/PROGRESS:
- Level up
- Step up
- Move up the ranks
- Advance to

DECLINE/WORSEN:
- Fall behind
- Lose ground
- Slip back
- Go downhill
```

**Idioms & Expressions:**
```
"That's not rocket science" = It's not that difficult
"It's a piece of cake" = It's easy
"You're barking up the wrong tree" = You're pursuing wrong direction
"The ball is in your court" = Your turn/responsibility
"Don't put all your eggs in one basket" = Diversify
"Break the ice" = Start conversation in awkward situation
"At the end of the day" = In conclusion
"It goes without saying" = Obviously
```

**Context Matters:**
```
FORMAL: "The project was ultimately completed."
INFORMAL: "We finally wrapped up the project."
CASUAL: "We got the project done, yo!"
```

#### Speaking Practice (20 min)

**Exercise 1: Phrasal Verb in Context**
- Teacher provides definition
- Student creates sentence with phrasal verb
- Natural, conversational setting
- Example: "Define 'back out'" → Student: "When she found out the cost, she decided to back out of the plans."

**Exercise 2: Idiom Recognition & Use**
- Teacher uses idiom in context
- Student identifies meaning
- Student creates own sentence with same idiom
- Repeat with 8-10 idioms

**Exercise 3: Formal → Informal Translation**
- Teacher: "The committee decided to postpone the meeting."
- Student: "Yeah, they decided to push the meeting back."

**Exercise 4: Conversational Practice with Idioms**
- Teacher and student have 5-minute conversation
- Student uses 5+ phrasal verbs/idioms naturally
- Sound conversational, not forced

#### Homework (5 min)
- Learn 10 new phrasal verbs + idioms
- Use each in a sentence (written)
- Record yourself using 5 in a short conversation
- Focus on natural delivery

### Lesson 10.2: Understanding What's NOT Said - Context & Implication

**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Level:** B1-B2

#### Warmup (5 min)
- Phrasal verb usage from 10.1

#### Core Content (30 min)

**English Communication: Direct vs. Indirect**

**The Unspoken Message:**

Many cultures value directness, but English-speaking cultures (especially British, Australian) often communicate indirectly.

**EXAMPLE 1: The Compliment That's Actually Criticism**
```
LITERAL: "That's an interesting choice."
SUBTEXT: "That's not a good choice, but I'm being polite."

LITERAL: "You're certainly confident."
SUBTEXT: "You're overconfident/arrogant."

LITERAL: "That's unique."
SUBTEXT: "That's weird/not good."
```

**EXAMPLE 2: The Polite Refusal**
```
DIRECT: "No, I don't want to."
INDIRECT: "That's very kind of you, but I'm not sure it's the right time."

DIRECT: "That won't work."
INDIRECT: "That's an interesting idea. Have you considered the challenges of...?"
```

**EXAMPLE 3: The Disagreement**
```
DIRECT: "You're wrong."
INDIRECT: "I see your point, though I might approach it differently."

DIRECT: "That's a bad idea."
INDIRECT: "That could work, but we might face some complications..."
```

**EXAMPLE 4: Status/Power in Language**
```
BOSS: "We should discuss this." (Command, sounds like suggestion)
EMPLOYEE: "Is it okay if we discuss this?" (Request, sounds uncertain)

Use of "might," "could," "perhaps" = less power
Use of "will," "must," "clearly" = more power
```

**Context Clues - Reading Between Lines:**

If someone says: "You must be very busy."
- In friendly tone = respect for your time
- In delayed tone = criticism (you're not making time for them)

If someone says: "That's great!"
- With enthusiasm = genuine
- With pause first = actually concerned
- Half-heartedly = not genuine

#### Speaking Practice (20 min)

**Exercise 1: Decode the Subtext**
- Teacher says phrase
- Student explains: What's actually being said?
- Student responds appropriately

Example:
- Teacher: "I'm sure you did your best."
- Student understanding: "That's probably not great work, but I'm being polite."
- Student response: "Thanks, though I know I could do better."

**Exercise 2: Say It Indirectly**
- Teacher: "Say this directly" → "Your presentation was boring."
- Student converts to indirect English: "The presentation had some interesting points, though I wonder if you might engage the audience more interactively."

**Exercise 3: Conversation with Subtext**
- Scenario: Boss is disappointed with employee's work
- Student plays employee
- Boss communicates disappointment indirectly (without saying "you did bad"
)
- Student reads between lines and responds appropriately

**Exercise 4: Catch Cultural Patterns**
- Listen to 3 different conversations
- Identify: Is this direct or indirect communication?
- What's actually being said?
- Why might speaker choose this approach?

#### Homework (5 min)
- Record a conversation where you:
  - Refuse something politely (indirectly)
  - Disagree with someone diplomatically
  - Express concern without being direct
- 3-4 minutes total
- Sound natural, not robotic

### Lesson 10.3: Humor, Sarcasm & Tone

**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Level:** B1-B2

#### Warmup (5 min)
- Subtext interpretation from 10.2

#### Core Content (30 min)

**Why Humor is Hard in ESL:**
- Cultural references don't translate
- Timing is critical
- Requires understanding nuance
- Can offend if misunderstood

**Types of Humor in English:**

**1. WORDPLAY (Puns)**
```
"I'm reading a book on the history of glue. I can't put it down."
(Can't put down = can't stop reading / can't put down = can't detach from)

"Time flies like an arrow. Fruit flies like a banana."
```

**2. IRONY/SARCASM**
```
A: "I got a B+ on my exam."
B: "Oh wow, what a disaster!" (obviously sarcasm - B+ is good)

A: "It's a beautiful day."
B: "Yeah, and I'm sure the weather will be perfect for our picnic." 
(Sarcasm - saying good thing when it's clearly bad weather)
```

**3. OBSERVATIONAL HUMOR**
```
"Have you noticed that nobody ever talks about how great shoelaces are? Just lost in the cracks..."
(Wordplay on "shoelace knots coming undone")
```

**4. SELF-DEPRECATING HUMOR**
```
"I tried meditation once. I did it wrong." 
(Self-mockery, safe and relatable)
```

**Understanding Tone (Critical for Sarcasm):**

**Same Words, Different Tone = Different Meaning:**

```
"Oh, that's great." (sincere) vs. "Oh, that's great." (sarcasm)
- Sincere: Excited tone, smile
- Sarcasm: Flat tone, eye roll
```

**Sarcasm Markers in English:**
- Exaggeration: "Oh SURE, I totally believe that."
- Pause: "Well... that's one way to do it."
- Tone change: Suddenly different pitch/speed
- Eye contact/expressions: Wink, raised eyebrow

#### Speaking Practice (20 min)

**Exercise 1: Recognize Sarcasm**
- Teacher says phrases with clear sarcasm
- Student identifies it's sarcasm
- Student explains what was actually meant

**Exercise 2: Deliver Sarcasm**
- Topic provided
- Student makes sarcastic remark
- Focus on tone, timing, context
- Not mean-spirited, but witty

Example:
- Context: "It's 6am and someone says they're a morning person"
- Sarcastic response: "Oh sure, and I'm a ninja turtle."

**Exercise 3: Self-Deprecating Humor**
- Student makes joke about themselves
- About language learning difficulty, cultural confusion, etc.
- Should be funny but not mean

**Exercise 4: Conversation with Humor**
- 5-minute conversation
- Student includes: one pun, one observation, one sarcasm
- Natural delivery
- Appropriate to context (not offensive)

#### Homework (5 min)
- Watch comedy clip (comedian speaking English)
- Take notes on humor types
- Record yourself explaining a joke + try telling it
- Reflect: What makes it funny? How is it different from your culture?

### Lesson 11.2: Academic Discourse & Sophisticated Discussion

**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Level:** B1-B2

#### Warmup (5 min)
- Specialized vocabulary from 11.1

#### Core Content (30 min)

**Academic English Characteristics:**

**1. PRECISE WORD CHOICE**
```
NOT: "I think people should eat healthier food."
ACADEMIC: "Evidence suggests that increased consumption of whole foods 
correlates with improved health outcomes."
```

**2. SOPHISTICATED SENTENCE STRUCTURE**
```
NOT: "Reading is good for you. It helps your brain."
ACADEMIC: "Reading stimulates cognitive development through multiple mechanisms: 
enhanced vocabulary acquisition, improved comprehension skills, and increased neural engagement."
```

**3. EVIDENCE-BASED REASONING**
```
NOT: "Most people work from home better."
ACADEMIC: "According to longitudinal research by Stanford University (2023), 
remote workers demonstrate 13% higher productivity rates when equipped with appropriate technology infrastructure."
```

**4. HEDGING IN ACADEMIC CONTEXT**
```
NOT: "This causes depression."
ACADEMIC: "This may contribute to depressive symptoms in certain populations, 
though causation remains contested in current literature."
```

**5. FORMAL REGISTER (Avoid contractions, casual words)**
```
NOT: "Yeah, so the thing is, kids aren't reading enough."
ACADEMIC: "Current trends suggest a decline in youth literacy engagement, 
particularly among adolescent populations."
```

**Academic Phrases:**

**PRESENTING EVIDENCE:**
- "Research demonstrates..."
- "Empirical findings suggest..."
- "Data indicates..."
- "Survey results demonstrate..."

**ACKNOWLEDGING COMPLEXITY:**
- "While this is compelling, counter-arguments suggest..."
- "The situation is more nuanced..."
- "It's important to acknowledge that..."

**MAKING CONCLUSIONS:**
- "In light of this evidence..."
- "One can infer that..."
- "This analysis points to..."
- "The preponderance of evidence suggests..."

#### Speaking Practice (20 min)

**Exercise 1: Translate to Academic**
- Teacher gives casual statement
- Student rewrites in academic English
- Delivers orally with proper register

Example:
- Casual: "Coffee makes you super alert but then you crash."
- Academic: "Caffeine stimulates the central nervous system, temporarily elevating alertness. However, subsequent metabolic breakdown can result in energy depletion."

**Exercise 2: Sophisticated Discussion**
- Topic from their field
- Student must discuss using:
  - Evidence-based claims
  - Hedging language
  - Formal register
  - Sophisticated structure
  - Acknowledgment of counter-arguments

**Exercise 3: Research Presentation**
- Student presents finding related to field
- 4-5 minutes
- Must include methodology, findings, implications
- Uses academic language throughout

**Exercise 4: Expert Interview**
- Teacher interviews student as expert in field
- Questions probe for:
  - Detailed knowledge
  - Awareness of current research
  - Nuanced understanding
  - Sophisticated explanation abilities

#### Homework (5 min)

## Advanced Level Teaching Notes

**Key Principles:**

**1. From Correction to Coaching**
- Less direct correction
- More questions: "What did you mean?" "Can you expand?"
- Let student self-correct through reflection

**2. Embrace Ambiguity**
- Advanced learners should explore nuance
- Not everything has one "right" answer
- Language varies by context, culture, region

**3. Push Toward Independence**
- Student should feel capable of:
  - Finding words they don't know
  - Managing conversations without support
  - Handling unexpected scenarios
  - Teaching others

**4. Celebrate Sophistication**
- When student uses complex structure correctly: "Great job with that dependent clause!"
- When student chooses precise word: "Perfect word choice!"
- Reinforce advanced communication skills

**5. Cultural Exchange**
- Advanced level is time for true cultural dialogue
- Student's culture enhances English understanding
- Explore differences in communication styles
- Build bridges, not just language
